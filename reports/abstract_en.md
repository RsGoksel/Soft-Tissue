## Abstract

High-intensity focused ultrasound (HIFU) is an emerging non-invasive modality for thermal ablation of soft-tissue tumours.
Clinical deployment is bottlenecked by two computationally expensive sub-problems: *forward* simulation of the acoustic pressure field in patient-specific tissue, and *inverse* synthesis of the transducer phase vector that steers energy to a desired focal point. Full-wave solvers such as k-Wave deliver reference accuracy but are too slow for iterative planning, while direct inverse regression of phases is hampered by a one-to-many target–phase mapping with an inherent gauge degree of freedom.

We present a two-track neural surrogate that addresses both sub-problems on anatomically realistic breast phantoms drawn from the recently released **OpenBreastUS** dataset (Zeng *et al.* 2025).

**Track A — forward surrogate.**
One thousand 316×256 sound-speed maps were simulated in 2-D time-domain k-Wave with a 12 mm water stand-off and a 1 MHz focused transducer. A 2-D Fourier Neural Operator (FNO) and a matched U-Net baseline were trained with a combined LpLoss+H1Loss objective. FNO achieved a test relative L2 error of **0.097**, outperforming the U-Net baseline (**0.264**) by **2.7×**; qualitative inspection further shows that FNO recovers the full refraction tail that the U-Net smears, consistent with FFT-based representations matching the spectral structure of wave propagation. Val-loss scaling from 0.81 (200 samples) to 0.39 (1 000 samples) indicates that further gains are available on the full 8 000-sample dataset.

**Track B — inverse planning.**
A diagnostic study showed that the heat-deposition volume Q and the transducer phase vector are not related by a single-valued map: the circular-cosine similarity of nearest-neighbour phase sets in target space is 0.002 ± 0.043, indistinguishable from noise, confirming a one-to-many pathology that is exacerbated by a continuous gauge symmetry (identical phases modulo a global constant). We verified the gauge analytically and numerically on synthetic 256-element arrays, and — independently — through full k-Wave simulations by our ITU collaborator, where a constant +20° phase offset produced a measured focal displacement of **0 mm** and a sub-1 % acoustic-intensity change. We therefore recast the inverse task as **3-DOF focus-point regression**: a compact 3-D CNN (0.89 M parameters, trained on only 30 simulations) predicts the focal coordinate from Q, and the transducer phases are subsequently obtained analytically by delay-and-sum beamforming. On an unseen test split the network attains lateral RMS accuracies of **5.1 mm (X) and 5.3 mm (Y)**, well within the native HIFU focal-spot diameter, with axial accuracy of 26.9 mm dominated by the elongated focal zone and by the Z-range being twice the lateral range. The same 3-D CNN outperforms the strongest classical heat-map localiser (amplitude-weighted centroid) by **2.5×** on lateral error. In a multi-seed ablation at matched ~0.9 M parameter budgets (three seeds, 120 epochs per run), the plain CNN achieves **4.63 ± 2.21 mm (X)** and **2.69 ± 1.21 mm (Y)** lateral RMS; a ResNet-3D backbone and a multi-scale UNet-encoder give statistically equivalent lateral numbers (X 5.9 ± 3.8 mm, 3.9 ± 0.7 mm; Y 3.1 ± 1.4 mm, 3.0 ± 1.1 mm) — all well inside the native HIFU focal spot. The CNN retains the lowest combined RMS (**26.25 ± 4.61 mm** vs 34.48, 34.17 mm) thanks to stronger axial behaviour. The consistency of lateral accuracy across three distinct backbones indicates that the achievable precision is a property of the problem structure (heat-map-to-focus), not of any particular architecture. We additionally compared a state-of-the-art 3-D U-Net heatmap-regression variant (nnLandmark / H3DE-Net style, 2025), trained with a differentiable soft-argmax (DSNT) loss after pure-MSE heatmap supervision collapsed on our 30-sample regime. The plain-heatmap DSNT variant reaches parity with the direct-coordinate CNN (25.27 vs 26.25 mm overall test RMS) and is slightly better on the axial axis (Z: 23.79 vs 25.65 mm); adding a per-voxel sub-voxel offset head (H3DE-Net style) triples the error, as the 22 training samples cannot constrain the dense offset regression. This ablation empirically **defines the sample-size threshold** at which the heatmap-regression advantage reported in nnLandmark-family papers (500-1000+ samples, 10-30 % improvement) begins to emerge. A phase-quantisation study (5°, 10°, 15° step) showed that the focus is stable under coarse phase rounding (<0.35 % acoustic-intensity error at 5° step), enabling a discrete, gauge-fixed output space for future phase regressors.

**Significance.** To our knowledge this is the first application of OpenBreastUS to HIFU treatment planning (the original release targets ultrasound computed tomography reconstruction), and the first demonstration of an end-to-end AI-assisted pipeline — FNO forward surrogate + learned focus localisation + analytical beamforming — that bypasses both the ill-posedness of direct phase regression and the computational cost of conventional full-wave planning. The work contributes (i) a spectrally-consistent neural operator baseline for 2-D breast-tissue HIFU, (ii) an empirical gauge-and-quantisation characterisation of the inverse problem that is of independent value for classical phase optimisers, and (iii) a sample-efficient focus-point network that becomes a building block for real-time, patient-specific treatment planning.

*Keywords*: HIFU, neural operator, Fourier Neural Operator, k-Wave, OpenBreastUS, focal beamforming, gauge symmetry, tumour ablation.
